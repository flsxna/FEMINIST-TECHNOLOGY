    The History of Feminist Technology

    These women were pioneers and trailblazers in the field of science and technology, and their accomplishments set an example for subsequent female scientists and engineers, advancing the progress of gender equality.

    • Marie Curie (1867-1934)

      Madame Curie was a Polish-born French physicist and chemist. Together with her husband Pierre Curie, she discovered the element radium, for which she was awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1903. After this, she continued her research on radioactive substances, becoming the first person to win two Nobel Prizes (the second was for her daughter Elisabeth Curie).

    • Ada Lovelace (1815-1852)

      Lady Lovelace was a British mathematician and writer, and is also considered to be the world's first computer programmer. She worked with Charles Babbage, for whom she designed analytical machines and wrote some algorithms, and is considered a pioneer of computer programs.

      Mar Hicks

      Mar Hicks is a historian of technology, gender and modern Europe, notable for their work on the history of women in computing. Hicks is a professor at the Illinois Institute of Technology. In her book "Programmed Inequality", she focuses on the role of women in computing, particularly their contributions to the development of early computer systems and their subsequent exclusion from the field. The book also sheds light on the experiences of women who worked in the field, including their struggles for recognition and equal treatment, and the ways in which they navigated discriminatory practices and attitudes. Ultimately, "Programmed Inequality" offers a critical examination of the social and historical contexts of computing and the role of gender in shaping its development.
